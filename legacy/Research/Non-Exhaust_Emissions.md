Emission Sense: Non-Exhaust Emissions (Tyre & Brake Wear)

Historically, emission modeling focused entirely on the tailpipe. However, recent studies by the European Commission and the UK Air Quality Expert Group (AQEG) reveal a critical shift: Non-Exhaust Emissions (NEE)—specifically the friction dust generated by tires, brake pads, and road surface wear—now account for over 60% of all vehicular $PM_{2.5}$ and $PM_{10}$ in urban environments.

For a comprehensive "Emission Sense" calculator, NEE must be calculated separately from tailpipe emissions ($EF_{DB}$) and then added to the total $PM_{2.5}$ output.

1. The Physics of Non-Exhaust Particulates

Non-exhaust $PM_{2.5}$ is generated purely through mechanical friction:

Tyre Wear: As the rubber tread grips the asphalt, microscopic shear forces tear away volatile organic compounds (VOCs), microplastics, and heavy metals (like Zinc) used in tire vulcanization.

Brake Wear: When friction brakes are applied, the semi-metallic or ceramic brake pads are ground against an iron rotor. This creates airborne metallic flakes, specifically iron, copper, and barium dust.

Because this is a friction-based mass loss, the base emission rate is highly dependent on the Vehicle Size (which dictates the mass pressing down on the tires and the kinetic energy the brakes must dissipate).

3. The Modifiers: Tyres, Brakes, and Powertrains

The base NEE is modified by the specific hardware installed on the vehicle and the powertrain's braking mechanics.

A. Tyre Type Multiplier ($f_{tyre}$)

The compound of the rubber heavily dictates how quickly it degrades into particulate matter.

Eco / Low Rolling Resistance: $0.90\times$ (Harder compound, less friction, sheds less mass).

Standard: $1.00\times$

Old / Degraded: $1.15\times$ (Aged rubber is brittle and flakes off more easily under stress).

Performance / Winter: $1.20\times$ (Softer compounds designed for maximum grip shed material very rapidly).

B. Brake Type Multiplier ($f_{brake}$)

The physical enclosure of the braking system dictates how much brake dust actually escapes into the atmosphere.

Disc Brakes: $1.00\times$ (Open-air caliper design; pad dust is immediately blown into the atmosphere by cooling vents).

Drum Brakes: $0.80\times$ (Closed housing traps a significant portion of the heavy brake dust inside the drum, preventing it from immediately becoming airborne $PM_{2.5}$).

C. The EV Regenerative Braking Reduction ($f_{regen}$)

Electric Vehicles (EVs) and Hybrids use their electric motors to slow the car down by converting kinetic energy back into battery charge.
Because the physical friction brakes are used significantly less (often only during hard panic stops or below $10 \text{ km/h}$), they generate far less brake dust.

ICE (Internal Combustion): $1.00\times$ (Relies 100% on friction brakes).

EV / Hybrid (Regen): $0.90\times$ (A 10% reduction applied specifically to the brake wear component).
(Note: While EVs save on brake wear, their heavy battery packs often push them into a higher "Vehicle Size" class, increasing their Tyre Wear. This creates a complex but accurate balance in the equation).

4. The Mathematical Equation

Because tyres and brakes are separate physical systems influenced by different multipliers, they must be calculated independently and then summed.

$$E_{nee\_total(PM2.5)} = D \times \left[ \left(EF_{tyre} \times f_{tyre}\right) + \left(EF_{brake} \times f_{brake} \times f_{regen}\right) \right] \times 0.001$$

Where:

$E_{nee\_total(PM2.5)}$: Total Non-Exhaust $PM_{2.5}$ in grams.

$D$: Distance traveled in kilometers.

$EF$: Base emission factors in milligrams per kilometer.

$0.001$: Conversion factor from milligrams to grams.

5. Software Implementation Reference

Here is the Python logic to calculate and isolate the Non-Exhaust Emissions for a trip:

def get_non_exhaust_emissions(distance_km, vehicle_size="Medium", tyre_type="Standard", 
                              brake_type="Disc", is_ev=False):
    """
    Calculates PM2.5 emissions generated by tire and brake wear.
    Returns the value in grams.
    """
    
    # 1. Base Factors (mg/km) based on Vehicle Size
    base_factors = {
        "Small":  {"tyre": 5.0,  "brake": 3.0},
        "Medium": {"tyre": 8.0,  "brake": 5.0},
        "Large":  {"tyre": 12.0, "brake": 8.0},
        "Heavy":  {"tyre": 30.0, "brake": 20.0}
    }
    
    factors = base_factors.get(vehicle_size, base_factors["Medium"])
    ef_tyre = factors["tyre"]
    ef_brake = factors["brake"]
    
    # 2. Tyre Type Multiplier
    tyre_mults = {"Eco": 0.90, "Standard": 1.00, "Old": 1.15, "Performance": 1.20}
    f_tyre = tyre_mults.get(tyre_type, 1.00)
    
    # 3. Brake Type Multiplier
    brake_mults = {"Disc": 1.00, "Drum": 0.80}
    f_brake = brake_mults.get(brake_type, 1.00)
    
    # 4. EV Regenerative Braking Multiplier (Applies ONLY to brakes)
    # EVs receive a 10% reduction in brake wear
    f_regen = 0.90 if is_ev else 1.00
    
    # 5. Calculate components (in mg/km)
    tyre_pm25_mg_km = ef_tyre * f_tyre
    brake_pm25_mg_km = ef_brake * f_brake * f_regen
    
    # Total NEE in mg/km
    total_nee_mg_km = tyre_pm25_mg_km + brake_pm25_mg_km
    
    # 6. Convert to total grams over the trip distance
    total_grams_pm25 = distance_km * (total_nee_mg_km * 0.001)
    
    return {
        "total_nee_pm25_grams": round(total_grams_pm25, 4),
        "tyre_contribution_grams": round(distance_km * tyre_pm25_mg_km * 0.001, 4),
        "brake_contribution_grams": round(distance_km * brake_pm25_mg_km * 0.001, 4)
    }
